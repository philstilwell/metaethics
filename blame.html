What does “basic desert” mean?

Basic desert is the claim that a person should receive blame, pain, or punishment simply because of what they knowingly did—not because the response protects anyone, repairs anything, changes behavior, communicates a boundary, or serves another human purpose.

The lab focuses narrowly on negative desert: whether suffering itself becomes fitting. It does not deny that an action happened, that a person caused it, that intentions matter, or that people may need to answer questions and make repair.

Does moral non-realism automatically remove moral responsibility?

No. Moral non-realism concerns moral facts or the meaning of moral claims. Skepticism about basic-desert responsibility concerns the control and justification needed for deserved blame. A person can accept one view and reject the other.

This lab combines them only as a conditional exercise: suppose no approval-independent moral fact makes suffering deserved. What practical responses can still be defended from evidence, commitments, relationships, agreements, and human goals?

Is every painful consequence a punishment?

No. Ending a friendship, removing access to a dangerous machine, or requiring repayment may predictably feel bad. The crucial question is whether the pain is the aim, an avoidable extra, or a regrettable cost of protection, repair, or another stated purpose.

Purpose does not provide a blank check. A restriction should use reliable evidence, choose the least harmful effective option, receive review, and end when its purpose no longer applies.

Does retribution always mean cruelty or revenge?

No. Retributive theories differ. Many require proof, proportionality, equal treatment, and strict limits; some emphasize formal censure or a fitting burden rather than a wish to maximize pain. A person can also feel anger without accepting any theory of deserved punishment.

This lab isolates a narrower claim: after every protective, preventive, reparative, reforming, relational, and public purpose has been removed, can deliberately added suffering still be justified because the person deserves it? The zero-purpose control tests exactly that claim.

What will the result bars mean?

Each case answer names exactly one primary response. The result shows how often you selected each response divided by how many cases offered it. If rehabilitation appears in five cases and you choose it twice, the bar reads 2 / 5.

The bars are not moral grades, truth probabilities, or personality scores. Choosing several responses is expected. The report flags only exact policy mismatches and any place where retributive suffering re-enters the supposedly desert-free exercise.

The main lesson

Removing intrinsic desert does not remove responsibility for danger, repair, trust, boundaries, or change.

It changes the question. Instead of asking how much suffering a person deserves, ask what response serves an identified purpose, what evidence supports it, who bears its costs, which less harmful option would work, and when the response must end. Retribution is different because it treats suffering itself as part of the point.
