Construction is not discovery

Human-made rules can do real work.

The hard part is saying what kind of work—and what gives anyone a reason to accept the result.

01

Facts remain

Pain, hunger, trust, scarcity, disagreement, and the effects of a rule can be investigated without objective moral properties.

02

Values remain

People can care, commit, bargain, protest, protect one another, and revise plans without claiming that their values exist outside every mind.

03

Procedures matter

Who participates, what information they receive, and whether they can refuse can change what an agreement actually shows.

04

Power is separate

Force can make a rule effective. It does not by itself explain why the person being forced has a reason to endorse the rule.

Does “without moral facts” mean that anything goes?

No. The premise removes one possible kind of fact: an objective moral status that exists independently of every person, agreement, practice, or goal. It does not remove injury, fear, preference, evidence, law, promises, cooperation, or power.

People can still give reasons that begin with things they or others care about. They can also expose false predictions, broken agreements, unfair procedures, hidden costs, and rules that defeat their own stated aims.

Is every constructed code a form of metaethical constructivism?

No. Philosophers use “constructivism” for several views. Many constructivists say there are genuine normative truths, but those truths depend on what agents would choose or agree to under carefully specified conditions rather than on an independent moral realm.

This simulator is narrower and more practical. It borrows the idea of making procedures explicit, but it does not classify your code as a philosophical theory or assume that the procedure creates a universal obligation.

What will the final bars mean?

Each answer openly names one or more bases: mutual benefit, fair agreement, shared commitments, informed preferences, harm reduction, democratic voice, coordination, or coercive power. The bars count how often you selected each basis when it was available.

They are not grades, truth probabilities, or measures of moral worth. Overlap is expected. A working code can appeal to several reasons at once, and the diagnostic will identify exact places where those reasons pull toward different responses.

The main lesson

A code can be built, defended, tested, and repaired without being discovered in a moral realm.

Construction does not make every code equal. You can compare codes by their stated aims, evidence, inclusion, consistency, effects, revision rules, and treatment of people with less power. What construction alone does not supply is an approval-independent command that every possible dissenter is objectively required to obey.
