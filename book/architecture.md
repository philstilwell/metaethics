# Book Architecture

## Final title

**The Objective Morality Project**  
*An Expedition to Build a Moral System Everyone Has Reason to Obey*

Titles considered during development:

- *Building Moral North*
- *The Last Moral System*
- *The Moral Constitution*
- *No Compass from Nowhere*
- *The Authority We Could Not Find*

## One-sentence description

A reader who expects objective morality to be constructible is led through a sustained, exercise-driven attempt to build it, repair it, and apply it—until every remaining candidate either loses objectivity or fails to become a coherent, knowable, authoritative system—after which the reader learns a practical method for moving from shared human emotions and values to revisable social policy.

## Scope and current length

- Complete main text, including embedded exercises and inspections: approximately 128,150 words
- Complete appendices and reusable worksheets: approximately 19,050 words
- Notes and bibliography: approximately 2,900 words
- Finished publication text with front matter: approximately 151,100 words; the current 7 × 10 inch print proof is 528 pages

The book should feel long because the reader performs the construction, not because the prose repeats the thesis.

## The cumulative argument

The argument unfolds in six movements.

1. **Specification.** Ordinary moral talk combines several aspirations: truth, universality, authority, knowability, consistency, and action guidance. The reader states which of these an objective moral system must possess.
2. **Construction.** The reader supplies a domain and grounds of direct standing, objects of judgment, values, definitions, verdict types, generation rules, reasons, methods for comparing unlike values, empirical inputs, and revision rules.
3. **Authority audit.** The book separates motivation, social pressure, legal force, conditional commitments, and practical support from the approval-independent authority the system promised.
4. **Access and stress tests.** The reader asks what could give evidence normative force before using evidence to test moral knowledge. Major moral families and hybrids then receive their strongest repairs. Each can preserve important human concerns, but none preserves the full specification without an unpaid bridge or a sacrificed aspiration.
5. **Conclusion.** A general grounding analysis shows why the failures recur. A foundation must be stance-dependent, non-evaluative, brute-evaluative, rationally constitutive, authoritative, or hybrid. Each route either stops short of objective moral content or recreates one of the debts already exposed. No coherent objective moral system has been found.
6. **Redirection.** Human life retains pain, pleasure, fear, attachment, trust, betrayal, agency, conflict, evidence, moralized emotions, institutions, and power. Rather than seeking doctrinal compromise among moral systems, public reasoning should begin from shared or mutually legible emotional and valuational primitives and translate them into evidence-sensitive, power-aware, revisable policy.

The conclusion is not “nothing matters.” It is that mattering begins in valuing creatures and their forms of life, not in a moral status hovering beyond every stance.

# Prologue — The Blank Constitution

**Purpose:** Invite the reader to construct, not merely evaluate. Establish a disaster-recovery assembly in which people with rival religions, cultures, and philosophies need a public constitution. They want more than a truce: they want the correct moral foundation.

**Narrative question:** What would have to be written on the first page so that every person is genuinely bound, including the dissenter, the outsider, the secret violator, and the person who loses under the rules?

**Reader artifact:** Open the Candidate System notebook and write a first sentence beginning, “An action is objectively right when …”

**Turn:** The blank page is not ignorance to be filled with the author's doctrine. It is the construction site.

# Part I — Specify the Thing You Hope to Build

This part prevents the inquiry from drifting between meanings of “objective,” “moral,” “true,” “binding,” and “coherent.” It also prevents the eventual negative conclusion from being produced by a hidden definition.

## Chapter 1 — The Promise

**Driving question:** What do people hope objective morality will give them that laws, preferences, customs, and negotiated agreements do not?

**Core distinctions:** confidence versus support; verdict versus system; seriousness versus objectivity; convergence versus truth.

**Construction task:** Record five expectations of the finished system. The default list is independence from approval, universal scope, genuine practical authority, knowability, and guidance through conflict.

**Pressure case:** A private act condemned by everyone but never discovered. What work is current condemnation doing after every social consequence is removed?

**Debt entry:** The system must explain what its verdict adds to factual descriptions of harm, preference, rule, reaction, and power.

## Chapter 2 — What “Objective” Must Mean

**Driving question:** Which forms of independence matter?

**Core distinctions:** mind-independent objects; stance-independent truth; impartial procedure; intersubjective stability; universality; observer-independence; response-independence.

**Construction task:** Select the minimum independence conditions the system must meet. State what would count as changing the subject.

**Pressure case:** Every actual and idealized person approves a rule because they share the same engineered preference. Does unanimity make the rule objectively true?

**Debt entry:** Consensus, impartiality, and stability may be valuable without constituting stance-independent truth.

## Chapter 3 — What Are You Doing When You Say “Wrong”?

**Driving question:** Is a moral sentence reporting a fact, voicing opposition, prescribing a plan, applying a standard, reporting an idealized construction, or participating in a useful fiction?

**Core distinctions:** semantics versus psychology; belief versus attitude; literal assertion versus practical use; truth relative to a standard versus truth about an objective status.

**Construction task:** Give the Candidate System a semantic rule for its central verdict terms.

**Exercises:**

- Translate “betrayal is wrong” into six possible speech acts.
- Decide whether two speakers can agree in verdict but disagree in meaning.
- Revisit one personal moral claim and write exactly what its utterance commits you to.

**Debt entry:** A word cannot switch between fact, command, attitude, and local rule whenever one interpretation is challenged.

## Chapter 4 — The Specification Sheet

**Driving question:** What properties must the completed system possess simultaneously?

**Proposed conditions:**

- **Truth:** at least some verdicts are apt to be correct or incorrect.
- **Stance independence:** correctness is not constituted merely by actual approval.
- **Scope:** the system says whom it reaches and why.
- **Authority:** it explains what counts in favor for a rejecting agent.
- **Accessibility:** agents have a defensible route to its central truths.
- **Coherence:** its claims and exception rules do not conflict opportunistically.
- **Determinacy:** it can settle enough cases to perform its promised work.
- **Usability:** finite, biased, time-limited agents can apply it.
- **Correctability:** error in belief or application can be identified and repaired.

**Construction task:** Mark each condition essential, negotiable, or unnecessary. For every negotiable condition, state how much can be lost before the project ceases to be objective morality.

**Pressure case:** Compare a perfectly coherent cruel code, a humane but internally contradictory code, and a true principle no one can know or apply.

**Debt entry:** Coherence is necessary for the stated project but is neither truth nor humaneness.

## Chapter 5 — One Argument, Eight Disagreements

**Driving question:** When moral opponents disagree, what exactly is in dispute?

**Layers:** wording and framing; empirical prediction; whose interests have standing; priority among admitted values; attitudes and commitments; coordination rules; decision procedures; alleged objective truth.

**Construction task:** Take a live disagreement and diagram its layers without deciding who is morally correct.

**Pressure case:** Two people utter opposite verdicts about assisted dying while agreeing on every empirical prediction. Additional information reveals that one dispute concerns standing, another priority, and another objective truth. The reader must not import unstated disagreement.

**Debt entry:** Evidence may resolve some layers without resolving priorities or ultimate status; a vote may settle policy without settling truth.

### Inspection Report I — The Bill of Specifications

The reader copies the final success conditions onto a one-page contract. Later repairs may change the contract, but each change must be marked as a surrender, clarification, or discovery. This report also records the Candidate System's current semantic rule.

# Part II — Give the System Content

The reader now constructs the visible architecture of a moral system. The aim is not to prefer one familiar theory but to reveal the selection decisions every theory must make.

## Chapter 6 — Draw the Circle

**Driving question:** Who or what counts directly?

**Possible grounds:** felt experience; agency and self-direction; interests that can be helped or harmed; relationship and dependence; membership and participation; approval-independent moral status; a defensible constructed inclusion rule; or denial of direct standing on the facts supplied.

**Candidates:** current people, future people, sentient animals, rational agents, infants, cognitively disabled people, communities, species, ecosystems, artificial minds, the dead, outsiders, and enemies.

**Construction task:** Draw the Candidate System's domain and distinguish direct standing, indirect relevance, and no standing.

**Pressure cases:** a short-lived sentient animal without plans; an isolated newborn; an advance directive after consciousness ends; conscious software; possible future people; a coordinated collective agent with no single conscious subject; and an ecosystem with no conscious or goal-directed subject.

**Boundary discipline:** Direct standing, comparative weight, and the final outcome are three questions. To count for one's own sake is not automatically to count equally, receive identical treatment, or defeat every competing concern.

**Debt entry:** The property used to draw the circle must be defended without excluding inconvenient cases or expanding opportunistically.

## Chapter 7 — Decide What Receives a Verdict

**Driving question:** Are we evaluating actions, motives, outcomes, rules, institutions, characters, relationships, or forms of life?

**Core distinctions:** act evaluation versus agent evaluation; justification versus excuse; illegality versus blameworthiness; tragedy versus wrongdoing; repair-demanding versus punishable.

**Construction task:** Create a status vocabulary with at least eight non-equivalent verdicts and rules for their use.

**Pressure case:** A clinician makes a reasonable decision from incomplete evidence and produces a catastrophic result. The reader must assign separate statuses to the act, process, intention, outcome, and institutional response.

**Debt entry:** “Wrong” cannot function as a universal bucket for harmful, forbidden, regrettable, punishable, dishonest, disloyal, and tragic.

## Chapter 8 — Name What Matters

**Driving question:** Which values enter, and what kind of value are they?

**Value families:** welfare and outcome; rule and duty; rights and autonomy; care and relationship; character and integrity; fairness and procedure; loyalty and belonging; authority and tradition.

**Construction task:** Choose primary, protected, and defeasible values. Identify whether each has value intrinsically, instrumentally, constitutively, relationally, or symbolically.

**Pressure cases:** emergency sacrifice; a dead person's synthetic voice; a friend's cheating; animal suffering versus extinction; memory erasure; a conscious replica facing deletion.

**Key lesson:** A mixed value profile is not incoherent merely because it is plural. Incoherence begins when the system cannot explain priority, scope, or changed treatment.

**Debt entry:** Listing values does not rank them or establish why they matter objectively.

## Chapter 9 — Turn Values into Guidance

**Driving question:** How does “this matters” become “do this”?

**Generation routes:** maximize; constrain; protect; embody; care for; honor; distribute fairly; follow legitimate authority; negotiate; construct through a procedure.

**Construction task:** For every value, write a generation rule and at least one exception rule.

**Pressure case:** Trust matters. Does that generate a ban on deception, a presumption, a virtue, a relationship-specific demand, a social policy, or a reason to compare consequences?

**Debt entry:** The transition from value to rule contains selectors about thresholds, agents, time, risk, and priority. Those selectors cannot remain invisible.

## Chapter 10 — Choosing Without a Common Measure

**Driving question:** What can make one choice better supported when protecting something important sets back something else important?

**Comparison methods:** a common-scale total; extra priority to the worse off; a protected boundary; a boundary with an emergency threshold; lexical or discontinuous priority; plural contextual judgment; a fair decision procedure; and a chosen or negotiated settlement without an objective ranking.

**Core distinctions:** comparing values; settling what will be done; and proving that one value is objectively greater. A procedure may settle action without completing the comparison. A commitment may guide action without discovering a universal order.

**Pressure cases:** one million mild headaches versus one permanent loss of sight; universal memory privacy versus preventing serious injuries; a relied-upon promise versus five additional lives; unique cultural achievement versus temporary illness; one death versus forty severe but temporary illnesses; two equally central but unlike abilities; and jobs, homes, local self-rule, sentient animals, and species loss.

**Construction task:** Give the Candidate System a comparison protocol. State the unit, priority, boundary, threshold, secondary rule, procedural limit, or chosen commitment that makes each decision possible.

**Debt entry:** No method receives a free tie-breaker. Aggregation needs a unit; priority needs a strength; constraints need scope; thresholds need placement; lexical ranks need same-rank rules; plural judgment needs discipline; procedure needs limits; chosen settlement must disclose commitment and power.

## Chapter 11 — Like Cases, Exceptions, and Identity

**Driving question:** What makes two cases relevantly similar?

**Core distinctions:** consistency versus uniformity; role-based difference versus favoritism; relationship-generated responsibilities versus identity alone; exceptions versus ad hoc escape.

**Construction task:** Write an identity-neutral rule, apply it to insiders and outsiders, then state all permitted relevance differences.

**Pressure case:** A person protects a friend from a rule they would enforce against a stranger when need, risk, evidence, promise, and consequences are identical.

**Debt entry:** “Relevant difference” is not self-interpreting. A system must say what makes a feature relevant without simply restating the desired verdict.

### Inspection Report II — The First Constitution

The reader assembles the grounds and boundaries of standing, object set, value table, status grammar, generation rules, comparison protocol, and exception criteria into a two-page constitution. A red-team worksheet searches for omitted beings, standing/weight collapse, overloaded terms, unsupported transitions, hidden common measures, incomplete tie-breakers, and asymmetric exceptions.

# Part III — Find the Source of the “Ought”

This is the book's central maze. The reader has built a system with content, but content is not authority. The chapters separate the different things ordinarily called reasons or obligations.

## Chapter 12 — Three Different Things Called a Reason

**Driving question:** Does a consideration explain an action, motivate the agent, or genuinely count in favor of the action?

**Construction task:** Classify background causes, motivating beliefs, and favoring considerations in a case where the agent acts from a false belief while an unknown fact supports another action.

**Pressure case:** A sensor failure causes an alarm; a false belief sends a person toward danger; an unknown breathable route would save them.

**Debt entry:** Causation, motivation, and practical support can overlap, but none may silently substitute for another.

## Chapter 13 — Current Aims and Better-Informed Aims

**Driving question:** Can an agent's desires generate all of their reasons?

**Candidates:** present desire; coherent desire; fully informed desire; desire after vivid appreciation; desire integrated with long-term identity.

**Pressure cases:** an implanted desire; voluntary loss of a future capacity; a fully informed and coherent tyrant.

**Construction task:** State which transformations count as correcting an agent rather than replacing their values.

**Debt entry:** Idealization needs a standard. If the standard is chosen because it produces humane answers, the humane selector is doing the real work.

## Chapter 14 — Commitments, Promises, and Roles

**Driving question:** Can voluntary commitment create a reason that survives current desire and detection?

**Pressure cases:** a promise to a person who later dies; a professional duty toward an enemy; a membership rule disclosed only after rescue.

**Construction task:** Define entry, scope, exit, emergency, deception, exploitation, and excessive-cost conditions for commitment-generated reasons.

**Debt entry:** A commitment can structure a life without binding every outsider or explaining why the act of choosing has objective authority.

## Chapter 15 — Fair Terms and Constructed Reasons

**Driving question:** Can a fair procedure generate reasons for those who lose or reject the outcome?

**Pressure cases:** a fair lottery that chooses against you; unanimous agreement made under unequal exit options; a procedure whose participants exclude an affected outsider.

**Construction task:** Design a procedure and specify who participates, what information they possess, how bargaining power is neutralized, and what constrains the outcome.

**Debt entry:** The choice of fairness conditions, participant set, vetoes, and outcome constraints imports evaluative selectors. Procedure organizes them; it does not summon them from nowhere.

## Chapter 16 — Reasons That Apply Whether You Care or Not

**Driving question:** Can suffering, agency, evidence, or another fact count in favor for a person wholly unmoved by it?

**Pressure cases:** the enemy in preventable pain; the unmoved antidote holder; the coherent tyrant who understands every consequence.

**Construction task:** Write the strongest account of an aim-independent practical reason and distinguish it from an objective moral fact.

**Fork:**

- If such reasons exist, explain their source, application, weight, and knowability.
- If they do not, explain criticism of coherent cruelty without borrowing their language.

**Debt entry:** Renaming an objective moral demand “a practical reason” does not pay the grounding debt.

## Chapter 17 — From Approval to Obligation

**Driving question:** What does social acceptance actually establish?

**Ladder kept non-ordinal:** personal commitment; shared code; social pressure; punishment or law; limited role or agreement; an added foundation; approval-independent duty.

**Pressure cases:** a secret forbidden song; towns with opposite disclosure rules; a reformer condemned in her own time; a harmless outsider who rejects ritual conformity.

**Construction task:** For each case, state separately what the rule establishes about classification, motivation, prudence, legal status, membership, and objective authority.

**Debt entry:** Force can secure obedience. Approval can define a local code. Neither fact alone produces approval-independent authority.

## Chapter 18 — God, Nature, and Rational Necessity

**Driving question:** Can authority enter from beyond human approval?

**Candidates:** divine command; divine nature; natural law; species flourishing; rational agency; constitutive standards of action; self-evident axioms.

**Steelman requirement:** Each candidate is presented first as an attempt to avoid arbitrariness, not as superstition or semantic trickery.

**Pressure tests:** interpretation under sincere disagreement; whether goodness explains authority or authority defines goodness; movement from function to value; rational consistency without substantive ends; the agent who rejects the allegedly constitutive aim.

**Debt entry:** An authority can issue commands, a nature can have functions, and rationality can constrain means. The system still owes the bridge to universal moral status and the method for identifying its content.

### Inspection Report III — The Missing Bridge

The reader fills a seven-column authority audit: commitment, convention, pressure, sanction, conditional reason, practical support, objective obligation. Every clause in the Candidate System is assigned to the strongest column it has earned. Unsupported clauses do not disappear; they are marked as promissory notes.

# Part IV — Ask How the System Could Be Known

Even a real moral order cannot guide if no method distinguishes discovery from projection. This part tests epistemic access without claiming that causal influence automatically refutes belief.

## Chapter 19 — Does Evidence Have Authority?

**Driving question:** A consideration may reliably indicate truth, but what—if anything—makes that relation a reason to believe?

**Possible sources:** truth-directed evidence; the constitutive aim or function of belief; an agent's truth-seeking project; practical usefulness; accepted rules of inquiry; a defensible public procedure; or rejection of any literal epistemic ought.

**Core distinctions:** truth versus justification; evidence versus a cause of belief; a belief's function versus a person's desire; reliable method versus lucky truth; private support versus publicly usable justification; moral skepticism versus epistemic anti-normativism.

**Pressure cases:** a comforting false belief with no harmful consequence; a useless mathematical truth; unanimous experts who prefer a forecast rejected by a better instrument; a lucky conspiracy belief; forgotten but once decisive evidence; a beneficial placebo belief; and a perfectly competent agent indifferent to truth and consistency.

**Construction task:** State why the book's reader should follow evidence during the remaining inquiry. The answer may be stance-independent, constitutive, desire-dependent, instrumental, practice-based, procedural, or openly anti-normativist.

**Boundary:** Objective moral skepticism does not establish or refute objective epistemic reasons. The moral argument may be conditional on truth-seeking aims and inquiry rules even if no stance-independent epistemic ought is assumed.

**Debt entry:** Saying that evidence bears on truth explains an important relation. It does not by itself explain why truth normatively governs an agent who rejects every truth-seeking aim.

## Chapter 20 — Intuition and the Feeling of Obviousness

**Driving question:** What is an intuition evidence of?

**Core distinctions:** seeming; confidence; emotional salience; trained perception; cultural fluency; conceptual competence; intellectual insight.

**Construction task:** Record three “obvious” judgments and list what could change each one.

**Pressure case:** Equal confidence attaches to opposite intuitions in agents with different formation histories.

**Debt entry:** Calling a response self-evident identifies its phenomenology, not yet a truth-tracking mechanism.

## Chapter 21 — Where Values Come From

**Driving question:** What follows from the genealogy of a conviction?

**Influence families:** harm sensitivity; reciprocity; kinship and group loyalty; religion; parents and community; status incentives; historical institutions; personal experience; deliberate reflection.

**Inference discipline:** causal explanation; local process distrust; content-level argument; broad burden shift.

**Construction task:** Build a tentative genealogy of one conviction without converting influence ratings into a truth score.

**Debt entry:** A causal origin does not entail falsehood. But a claimed faculty of moral knowledge owes an explanation of why its formation is appropriately connected to stance-independent truth.

## Chapter 22 — Disagreement and Convergence

**Driving question:** Does persistent disagreement count against objective morality?

**Steelman:** Disagreement exists in domains with objective truth; convergence can be produced by power, shared biology, or common incentives.

**Construction task:** Separate tractable empirical disagreement from disagreement over framing, standing, priority, attitude, and ultimate status.

**Pressure case:** Two populations converge after one controls education and punishment. Another pair continues to disagree despite equal evidence and ideal reflection.

**Debt entry:** Disagreement is not a refutation, but an epistemology must explain expected convergence, rational peer conflict, and correction.

## Chapter 23 — Facts Enter; Values Select

**Driving question:** What can empirical evidence settle?

**Core distinctions:** predicted consequences versus their priority; measurement versus standing; descriptive function versus proper function; scarcity versus distribution; risk estimate versus acceptable risk.

**Construction task:** Trace a policy claim from data through every evaluative selector to the verdict.

**Pressure case:** All parties agree on a policy's effects but differ over whose loss should control and which risks are acceptable.

**Debt entry:** More facts can expose mistakes and constrain means, but facts do not silently choose the terminal criterion.

## Chapter 24 — Novel Cases, Uncertainty, and Correction

**Driving question:** How does an objective system respond when its application is uncertain or its concepts are new?

**Cases:** artificial consciousness; memory editing; synthetic identity; population change; remote causal responsibility; ecological thresholds.

**Construction task:** Add rules for uncertainty, evidence thresholds, precaution, appeal, and revision.

**Dilemma:** A truth can be fixed while beliefs change, but every humanly usable system needs revisable interpretation. What prevents revision from being opportunistic and fixed authority from becoming insulated?

**Debt entry:** “The truth never changes” does not specify how mistaken applications are detected or repaired.

### Inspection Report IV — The Access Audit

The reader must first state what gives evidence force within the inquiry, then identify what would count as evidence for and against each foundational claim. “Nothing could count against it” is permitted but recorded as insulation rather than confirmation. The report separates truth indication from epistemic authority, public procedure from truth, and failure of moral access from proof of moral nonexistence.

# Part V — Let the Great Candidates Make Their Best Case

The reader now applies the completed audit to major theory families. Each chapter begins with the problem that made the theory compelling and gives it its strongest natural refinements.

## Chapter 25 — Count the Consequences

**Attraction:** suffering and flourishing are real to the beings who experience them; outcomes cannot responsibly be ignored.

**Strong forms considered:** act and rule consequentialism; preference and welfare views; negative and prioritarian variants; plural goods; thresholds.

**Pressure points:** measurement; interpersonal comparison; aggregation; demandingness; separateness of persons; population ethics; hidden distribution; who counts.

**Repair audit:** Each refinement protects a value by adding a selector not supplied by maximizing alone.

**Surviving contribution:** disciplined attention to effects and counterfactuals.

## Chapter 26 — Respect the Rule and the Person

**Attraction:** individuals should not become mere containers in an aggregate calculation.

**Strong forms considered:** universalization; rights; side constraints; contractual duties; role duties; threshold deontology.

**Pressure points:** conflicting duties; description of maxims; status of non-rational beings; catastrophic thresholds; positive versus negative duties; hidden priority rules.

**Repair audit:** Exceptions must be generated rather than intuited after the verdict is desired.

**Surviving contribution:** explicit boundaries against instrumentalization and opportunistic exception.

## Chapter 27 — Character, Care, and Belonging

**Attraction:** actual life is relational, emotionally textured, and dependent on judgment that no algorithm captures.

**Strong forms considered:** virtue ethics; care ethics; relational duties; loyalty; stewardship; practical wisdom.

**Pressure points:** cultural exemplars; conflict among virtues; care as control; caregiver limits; partiality; outsiders; action guidance; formation before wisdom.

**Repair audit:** Practical wisdom integrates concerns but cannot be the unexplained faculty that always sees the right balance.

**Surviving contribution:** perception of vulnerability, relationship, character formation, and context.

## Chapter 28 — Receive the Moral Order

**Attraction:** a created or teleological order promises authority without human invention.

**Strong forms considered:** divine command, divine nature, natural law, objective human flourishing, sacred tradition with interpretive communities.

**Pressure points:** access; competing revelations; interpretation; authority versus goodness; function versus norm; dissent; outsiders; revision.

**Repair audit:** Moving authority from commands to a good nature avoids one arbitrariness but makes “good” carry the grounding burden.

**Surviving contribution:** durable communities, narratives of meaning, disciplined practice, and attention to human dependency—none of which by itself establishes objective moral truth.

## Chapter 29 — Build What No One Could Reasonably Reject

**Attraction:** rules should be justifiable to those they govern, not imposed from a private standpoint.

**Strong forms considered:** contractualism; ideal-observer views; Kantian and Humean constructivism; democratic legitimacy; reciprocal agreement.

**Pressure points:** who enters the procedure; idealization; adaptive preferences; power; unreasonable rejectors; constraints on outcomes; authority over nonparticipants.

**Repair audit:** A construction can be fair, stable, and supportable while remaining a human construction rather than a discovered stance-independent order.

**Surviving contribution:** public justification, representation, reciprocity, and procedural accountability.

## Chapter 30 — Let Standards Remain Local or Plural

**Attraction:** humility toward difference and resistance to imperial claims of universal truth.

**Strong forms considered:** culture-relative truth; agreement-relative reasons; contextualism; value pluralism; personal commitments; subcommunity standards.

**Pressure points:** society boundaries; overlapping communities; reformers; outsiders; criticism across time; internally oppressed groups; majority capture.

**Repair audit:** Internal criticism and plural standards preserve criticism but make “the society's morality” too simple. Approval-independent criticism reopens the objective-grounding debt.

**Surviving contribution:** explicit standpoint, historical awareness, and suspicion of false universality.

## Chapter 31 — The Hybrid Rescue

**Driving question:** Can a plural or layered system keep the strengths and avoid the failures?

**Candidate hybrids:** rights constrained by welfare thresholds; virtues guided by rules; care within fair procedures; divine aims interpreted through flourishing; plural values balanced by judgment; reflective equilibrium.

**Pressure tests:** who chooses the layers; what breaks ties; whether coherence is achieved by an undeclared master value; whether reflective equilibrium converges or merely systematizes starting commitments.

**Construction task:** Build the reader's strongest hybrid, state its ordering rules, and prohibit “all things considered” from serving as an unexplained answer.

**Debt entry:** A hybrid may be more humane and usable. It does not become objective merely by containing multiple incomplete grounds.

### Inspection Report V — What Each Candidate Saved

This report refuses winner-take-all comparison. The reader records the real human concern each theory protects, the price of its strongest repair, and the remaining objective claim it has not established. This becomes the raw material for the post-system alternative.

# Part VI — The Last Repairs and the General Result

This part does not simply repeat objections. It tests whether social criticism, responsibility, and code construction require the missing objective layer, then explains why the same grounding debts recur across candidates.

## Chapter 32 — Criticize the World You Inherited

**Driving question:** If current approval is not objective truth, what supports reform and dissent?

**Grounds kept distinct:** majority standards; subcommunity standards; internal inconsistency; prior promises; personal commitment; harm and agency; idealized agreement; alleged objective truth.

**Cases:** inherited caste; unanimous safety cover-up; judgment across time; overlapping communities; broken equal-citizenship promise; openly pro-conquest culture.

**Construction task:** State what each criticism establishes and what it leaves open.

**Result:** Effective criticism can expose facts, broken commitments, harms, domination, exclusions, and conflicts without first proving objective moral status. Its force may remain conditional on values, audiences, and power.

## Chapter 33 — Build a Public Code Without the Missing Layer

**Driving question:** What survives if no objective moral fact settles institutional design?

**Charter decisions:** primary goal; protected interests; decision procedure; dissent; membership; enforcement limit; revision; outsiders; protection against majority abuse.

**Possible supports:** mutual advantage; fair agreement; shared commitments; informed preferences; harm reduction; democratic legitimacy; stability; coercive power.

**Construction task:** Draft and pressure-test a community charter while labeling the basis of every clause.

**Result:** A code can be coherent, defensible to many, enforceable, and revisable without being objectively ordained. Support, compliance, and approval-independent obligation remain different.

## Chapter 34 — Power, Dissent, and the Person Who Still Says No

**Driving question:** What happens when persuasion and shared value fail?

**Core distinctions:** reason-giving; bargaining; coordination; legitimate procedure; legal jurisdiction; coercion; domination; force.

**Pressure cases:** the permanent dissenter; an outsider affected but unrepresented; a majority willing to sacrifice a minority; a powerful actor who can safely defect.

**Construction task:** Add safeguards, stopping rules, appeal, minority protections, and transparency while stating which values justify them.

**Result:** Political necessity does not become objective moral authority. Sometimes societies constrain people because others care intensely about the stakes and possess coordinated power. Honesty about this is not approval of arbitrary force; it is the start of controlling it.

## Chapter 35 — Responsibility Without Cosmic Payback

**Driving question:** What remains of blame if suffering is not deserved for its own sake?

**Response purposes:** protection; deterrence; restitution; rehabilitation; boundary-setting; withdrawal of trust; public condemnation; retributive suffering.

**Cases:** deliberate sabotage; sold memory; public rescue fraud; a fully reformed offender; coercion; organizational incentives; a zero-purpose unit of pain.

**Construction task:** Give every adverse response a purpose, safeguard, proportionality condition, and stopping rule.

**Result:** Removing basic desert does not remove causal agency, intention, danger, evidence, repair, trust, protest, or change. It removes one alleged reason for suffering.

## Chapter 36 — Moral Emotions Without Moral Desert

**Driving question:** What can support, redirect, regulate, or retire guilt, shame, resentment, indignation, gratitude, and pride when intrinsic desert is not assumed?

**Possible jobs:** tracking harm or benefit; answering to trust, dependence, and relationship; protesting and setting boundaries; supporting acknowledgment and repair; expressing commitments and coordinating expectations; regulating attention and action; or replacing a moralized emotion with regret, concern, caution, appreciation, grief, or another response.

**Core distinctions:** the cause of an emotion; the accuracy of its appraisal; its current function; whether and how it should be expressed; its fitting intensity; and its duration. An involuntary feeling can be understandable without being accurate, useful, or worth cultivating.

**Pressure cases:** guilt after a faultless accident and completed repair; learned shame about a harmless trait; resentment after serious betrayal and complete repair; indignation toward a vanished injustice with no remaining target or task; gratitude for a deliberately controlling gift; personal pride in an achievement one did not help produce; and remorse after abandoning belief in objective promise-breaking.

**Construction task:** For each emotion, identify the object, appraisal, audience, purpose, effect, safeguard, and stopping or revision condition.

**Result:** Removing intrinsic moral desert does not require emotional flattening. Recognition, relationship, protest, repair, expression, and self-regulation may survive. Some moralized forms may lose their support when they become self-punishment, conformity, manipulation, empty display, or punishment after every task is complete.

## Chapter 37 — No Candidate Returns Intact

**Driving question:** Is the recurring failure accidental, or does it follow from the kinds of foundations available?

**Grounding partition:** A system's decisive standard is grounded in one or more of the following:

1. an actual human stance, commitment, convention, or institution;
2. an idealized or procedurally constructed stance;
3. an authority's command or nature;
4. a non-evaluative natural fact;
5. a rational or conceptual requirement;
6. a brute evaluative fact or aim-independent reason;
7. a hybrid of these.

**General audit:**

- Actual stances and institutions can create practices, motives, classifications, incentives, and conditional reasons, but their content is not independent of stances.
- Idealization and procedure require selectors—whose standpoint, which information, what counts as rational correction, what constraints apply—and those selectors carry the disputed value.
- Authority can explain enforcement or obedience, but unless authority is already good or legitimate, its command does not supply moral content. If it is already good, goodness carries the debt.
- Natural facts constrain means and reveal consequences, capacities, functions, and needs. Movement from those facts to a terminal value requires an evaluative bridge.
- Rational requirements can expose inconsistency and means–end failure. Substantive moral content arrives only when ends, standing, or impartiality are added.
- Brute evaluative facts preserve objectivity by stipulation but owe an account of identity, access, authority, conflict, and projection. Calling them basic halts the explanation where the project most needed it.
- Hybrids inherit the debts of their parts and add an ordering debt.

**Epistemic boundary:** The audit does not infer moral non-realism from epistemic anti-normativism or infer epistemic anti-normativism from moral non-realism. Evidence may track truth, serve the constitutive function of belief, advance truth-seeking projects, support practical aims, discipline an inquiry practice, or provide public justification. The book's evidential conclusions remain available to readers who participate in any of those truth-responsive routes. A stance-independent epistemic reason, if one exists, does not supply the missing moral content by itself.

**Logical conclusion:** No route inspected yields a system that is simultaneously stance-independent, contentful, authoritative for rejectors, knowable, coherent through conflict, and usable without importing an unsupported selector. A coherent code can be built by lowering the objective ambition. An objective claim can be preserved by leaving decisive debts unpaid. No coherent objective moral system has been found.

**Epistemic restraint:** This is not a proof that no logically possible objective moral property exists. It is a demonstration that the construction project has no warranted completion and that continued assertion exceeds what has been established.

### Inspection Report VI — The Unfinished Constitution

The reader places the original specification beside the final candidate. Every surviving clause is marked as:

- factual;
- instrumental;
- expressive;
- commitment-based;
- agreement- or role-based;
- procedural;
- enforced;
- or objectively asserted but unpaid.

The blank space at the center is now understood, not merely felt.

# Part VII — After the Search

The final part must be expansive enough to prevent nihilism and concrete enough to change how readers approach political disagreement. It does not offer another total moral theory under a new name.

## Chapter 38 — Decide What to Do with Moral Language

**Driving question:** If ordinary moral claims overstate their grounding, should we stop, pretend, continue, revise, reconstruct, or translate?

**Options:** abolition; conscious fiction; conservation; expressivist revision; constructivist revision; ordinary-language pragmatism.

**Contexts:** promises; parenting; friendship; blame; criminal law; reform; public persuasion.

**Construction task:** Choose a context-sensitive language policy and state the risks of misunderstanding in each context.

**Result:** Rejecting objective facts does not itself select a language policy. Different uses may be more honest or useful in different settings.

## Chapter 39 — Everything That Did Not Disappear

**Inventory:** pain; pleasure; fear; attachment; grief; trust; betrayal; dependence; agency; humiliation; coercion; scarcity; evidence and truth-responsive practices; guilt; shame; resentment; indignation; gratitude; pride; causal responsibility; institutions; laws; agreements; identities; power; repair; imagination; hope.

**Driving question:** Which alleged losses were carried by objective morality, and which were present all along?

**Exercise:** Rewrite ten moralized sentences in terms of concrete stakes without weakening their emotional seriousness.

**Guardrails:** Non-realism does not entail equal preference strength, indifference, selfishness, legal abolition, inability to criticize, or skepticism about empirical truth.

## Chapter 40 — Begin with the Primitives

**Thesis:** Practical and political reasoning begins in creatures who care and react. Emotions and values are the primitives from which reasons are articulated, coalitions are formed, conflicts become salient, and policies acquire purposes.

**Primitive does not mean:** infallible; uncaused; universal; biologically fixed; immune from evidence; impossible to educate; automatically fit for law.

**Primitive families:** pain aversion; security; attachment; compassion; anger at betrayal; resentment of domination; disgust; loyalty; autonomy; curiosity; play; status; belonging; dignity; reciprocity; hope.

**Translation sequence:**

1. Name the felt response without moral inflation.
2. Identify the concrete stake it may be tracking.
3. Check the factual model through a stated truth-responsive method and listen to affected people.
4. State the value in operational terms.
5. Identify who shares it, who does not, and who bears the cost.
6. Design a policy aimed at that value.
7. Add safeguards against predictable power abuse.
8. Define evidence, review dates, failure conditions, and repair.

**Running case:** A city responds to youth violence. Fear points toward immediate safety; resentment points toward unequal enforcement; care points toward prevention and trauma; autonomy points against indiscriminate surveillance; belonging points toward trusted public space. The task is not to compromise among utilitarianism, rights theory, care ethics, and virtue ethics. It is to make these human stakes explicit and design testable policies.

## Chapter 41 — Stop Reconciling Moral Systems

**The admonition:** Stop beginning political inquiry by searching for doctrinal common ground between moral systems or by allocating each theory a portion of the compromise. The theories are downstream abstractions. Their apparent overlap often hides different meanings, authorities, constituencies, and priorities.

Begin instead with questions such as:

- What are people afraid will happen?
- Whose pain or loss is being noticed, and whose is invisible?
- What attachments or identities are threatened?
- Where do people experience domination, humiliation, betrayal, exclusion, or insecurity?
- Which concrete forms of agency, trust, care, belonging, stability, or freedom do they want protected?
- Which predictions are factual and testable?
- Which tradeoffs remain after the facts are agreed?
- Who has power to impose the settlement?
- What safeguards and revision paths make that power less dangerous?

**Why not “compromise among theories”:** A compromise between utilitarianism and deontology is not a constituency, an emotion, a need, or a policy objective. It can conceal the very stakes that made either theory attractive. Policies work on people and environments, not on philosophical systems.

**What commonality remains:** The method does search for shared emotions and values, but at the level of lived stakes rather than doctrinal propositions. Agreement may be partial. Some parties may not share the relevant concern. The method does not invent consensus. It makes coalition, bargaining, coercion, and irreducible conflict visible.

**Final exercise:** The reader takes one public controversy and produces a Civic Stakes Brief containing affected parties, emotional stakes, operational values, disputed facts, power map, policy options, safeguards, metrics, review, and repair. No sentence may rely on “moral,” “immoral,” “right,” “wrong,” “just,” “unjust,” “good,” “evil,” “duty,” or “desert” unless the term is immediately unpacked.

**Final line direction:** We did not earn a moral law above practical life. We found the beings who fear, need, love, resist, suffer, cooperate, and hope—and those were the beginnings we could identify, examine, and bring into public view.
