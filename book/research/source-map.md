## Complementary coherence framework

Phil Stilwell's field guide supplies one complementary structural audit. It receives equal weight with the suite concepts where it sharpens them, but it does not control the order, relative emphasis, or conclusion of the new book:

1. Domain — who counts?
2. Object — what is being judged?
3. Values — what matters?
4. Terms — what do the key words mean?
5. Statuses — what verdicts can be assigned?
6. Generation — how do values become guidance?
7. Reasons — what justification can be given?
8. Similarity — are like cases treated alike under the system's own criteria?
9. Conflict — how are collisions handled?
10. Facts — what empirical claims does the system need?
11. Guidance — can actual agents use it?
12. Origin — where did the system and its selectors come from?
13. Revision — can it be challenged, corrected, and repaired?

The new book distributes these conditions across the construction sequence rather than presenting them as a checklist first. Several suite concepts go beyond this list and must retain independent prominence: the different jobs performed by reasons; the layers of disagreement; the separation of support, compliance, and authority; responsibility without desert; post-error language policy; and the move from primitive emotions and values to public institutions.

## Coverage constraints

The manuscript must preserve these distinctions:

- moral realism versus first-order ethical theory;
- truth versus authority versus motivation;
- social acceptance versus enforcement versus conditional obligation;
- a local code versus a culture-relative truth claim;
- factual disagreement versus disagreement over standing or priority;
- explanation of a value versus defeat of the value;
- coherent institutional design versus objective justification;
- criticism of a society versus discovery of objective truth;
- responsibility practices versus basic desert;
- moral skepticism versus skepticism about all normative reasons;
- the absence of objective moral facts versus a decision about future moral language.

## Secondary literature already represented locally

The repository's reading room supplies a starting bibliography: C. L. Stevenson, Gilbert Harman, Bernard Williams, Simon Blackburn, Sharon Street, Richard Joyce, Richard Garner, Derk Pereboom, Gregg Caruso, David Plunkett and Timothy Sundell, François Jaquet, Hichem Naar, Sebastian Köhler, Michael Ridge, Björn Eriksson, Jonas Olson, Mark Schroeder, and the Stanford Encyclopedia entries on constructivism, relativism, practical reasons, and responsibility skepticism. These sources support accuracy and historical context; they do not determine the book's architecture.

Before publication, each substantive historical or interpretive claim must be checked against the primary work. The current architecture uses positions, not quotations.

## Originality constraints

- Do not reuse suite titles as chapter titles.
- Do not describe exercises as diagnostics or return profiles or scores.
- Do not preserve the suite's order.
- Do not reuse more than a small minority of its fictional cases verbatim.
- Convert recurring ideas into one continuous construction narrative.
- Make the final values-to-policy method more explicit and demanding than the suite's post-error material.
