# Adversarial Philosophical Review

## Review standard

The question is not whether every opponent will agree. It is whether a sophisticated opponent can recognize the strongest version of the position, locate the exact point of dispute, and see that the book’s conclusion does not claim more than the audit establishes.

## Strongest realist objections

### 1. The explanatory-demand objection

**Objection:** The book demands a nonnormative explanation of normativity and then declares normative foundations unpaid because they remain normative. That rigs the inquiry against basic reasons or values.

**Finding:** Chapters 16 and 37 already block the crude version. They explicitly grant that explanation stops, that basic evaluative facts need not be false or supernatural, and that reduction to nonnormative facts may be the wrong demand. The remaining questions concern content, scope, comparison, defeat, and access. No revision to the general conclusion is required.

### 2. Deliberative indispensability

**Objection:** Deliberation itself requires treating some considerations as genuine reasons. Trying to decide whether reasons exist already relies on them.

**Finding:** The manuscript discussed deliberation and constitutive standards but did not initially isolate the indispensability argument at full strength. Chapter 16 now gives it an explicit hearing. The reply is bounded: indispensability may support objective normativity while leaving substantive moral standing and priority open.

### 3. Companions in guilt

**Objection:** If objective moral reasons are rejected for queerness or access problems, objective epistemic reasons fall with them. The book cannot rationally argue for skepticism after destroying the normativity of evidence.

**Finding:** Chapters 19 and 37 treat this as a serious parity challenge. They distinguish truth indication, constitutive correctness, truth-seeking aims, inquiry commitments, public procedure, and stance-independent epistemic reasons. The argument proceeds conditionally with readers who accept truth-seeking and correction. It does not claim global normative impossibility.

### 4. Phenomenology and seemings

**Objection:** Moral seemings can provide defeasible prima facie justification without an independent oracle, just as perception does. Requiring external calibration is an excessive standard.

**Finding:** Chapter 20 recognizes noninferential justification and intellectual insight as live proposals. Its partial calibration protocol adjusts confidence and rival explanations; it does not insist that every justified belief be externally proven. The conclusion is limited to the failure of confidence alone to discriminate among explanations.

### 5. The moral-explanation objection

**Objection:** Objective moral facts may explain reform, deliberation, convergence, and the correctness of social criticism. Their explanatory role supplies reason to believe them.

**Finding:** The manuscript grants these as possible realist explanations but notes that stance-dependent concern, social formation, evidence, and institutional feedback can explain much of the same practice. The audit records underdetermination, not refutation. Chapter-level notes now point readers to Enoch, Shafer-Landau, and Cuneo for stronger positive realist arguments.

## Strongest first-order objections

### Consequentialism

**Objection:** Sophisticated consequentialism can include rules, rights, priority, equality, agency, and plural goods. Criticizing a single pleasure ledger is a straw person.

**Finding:** Chapter 25 constructs exactly these repairs and acknowledges that a humane consequentialism may be the best practical system. Its remaining challenge concerns the selected currency, distribution, thresholds, and objective authority—not a refusal to allow sophistication.

### Deontology

**Objection:** Kantian respect is not an arbitrary boundary pasted onto outcomes; it arises from rational nature and reciprocal lawgiving.

**Finding:** Chapters 18 and 26 present autonomy, universalization, persons as ends, maxims, positive duty, and rational status. The pressure case asks how non-rational beings enter and how formal requirements acquire substantive equal regard. The book should not describe Kant as merely rule worshiping; it does not.

### Virtue and care

**Objection:** Practical wisdom is not an algorithm with a missing cell. Demanding a decision procedure mistakes the point of cultivated perception.

**Finding:** Chapter 27 allows nonalgorithmic judgment and treats attention, relationship, formation, and emotional perception as genuine epistemic achievements. It asks for articulable features and correction because public coercion cannot rest only on the honorific *wise*. The distinction should remain.

### Divine nature and natural law

**Objection:** The book treats goodness as external to God and function as statistically normal behavior.

**Finding:** Chapters 18 and 28 explicitly reject both caricatures. They consider identity with necessary divine nature, intelligible basic goods, plural participation, diverse flourishing, conscience, tradition, and interpretation. Their debts concern identification and authority rather than conceptual incoherence.

### Constructivism and contractualism

**Objection:** The point of construction is not to discover a stance-independent realm. Validity may consist in what survives the right practical standpoint or reciprocal relation; asking for an external foundation changes the theory.

**Finding:** Chapters 15 and 29 accept that response. A coherent constructivist system can satisfy many practical and political aspirations. It falls short only of the stronger stance-independent target the reader may have placed on the original specification. The book marks that as a narrowed aspiration, not a refutation of constructivism.

### Relativism and pluralism

**Objection:** Relativism is not current-majority infallibility. Standards can be indexed to agreements, outlooks, historical confrontation, roles, or practices.

**Finding:** Chapters 17 and 30 distinguish those forms and preserve internal, cross-temporal, subcommunity, and external criticism. The boundary-selection problem remains, but the view is not reduced to polling.

## Strongest political objection

**Objection:** Beginning with common emotions and values can reproduce prejudice, majoritarian capture, propaganda, and the exclusion of beings unable to report their stakes.

**Finding:** Chapters 40–41 do not infer policy directly from emotion or consensus. They require appraisal testing, standing analysis, affected-party representation, distribution, comparison rules, power disclosure, alternatives, safeguards, measurement, review, sunset, and repair. When common concern fails, the text names bargaining, coercion, and force. The constructive ending survives the objection.

## Conclusion of review

No major theory family is dismissed by a beginner’s caricature. The book’s central negative result is best described as **failure to earn the complete specification through the examined routes**, not proof of metaphysical impossibility. That bounded formulation is present in Chapter 37, Appendix F, the architecture, and the support documents and must remain unchanged through copyediting and marketing.
