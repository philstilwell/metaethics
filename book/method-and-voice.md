# Method and Voice

## The reader contract

The book must not obtain its conclusion by defining objective morality so extravagantly that failure is automatic. At the beginning, the reader is asked to decide which familiar aspirations are essential and which are merely desirable. The manuscript then uses the reader's own declared aspirations as the standard of success.

The common core is:

- moral verdicts purport to be more than reports of what a person or group happens to approve;
- at least some verdicts remain true under universal disagreement;
- the system can explain why its truths have practical authority;
- its central claims are knowable by some defensible method;
- its rules do not contradict one another or change opportunistically;
- it can guide actual agents through conflict, uncertainty, and novel cases.

A reader may weaken this bundle. The manuscript must acknowledge when doing so produces a coherent constructivism, relativism, expressivism, practice, or code. The point is then that the reader has changed projects, not that the replacement is worthless.

## The steelman rule

Every major candidate receives:

1. its most attractive motivating insight;
2. a sophisticated formulation rather than a slogan;
3. the strongest natural repair to the first objection;
4. at least one case in which it performs better than rivals;
5. an explicit statement of what its failure does **not** prove.

No theory is rejected because its name sounds metaphysical, religious, sentimental, authoritarian, or fashionable. No appeal to evolution or disagreement is treated as an automatic refutation. No non-realist alternative is allowed to borrow objective authority after officially rejecting it.

## The anti-rigging rule

Pressure cases must state their fixed facts. A case may isolate one variable, but it may not quietly assume the conclusion. After each case, the reader receives three opportunities:

- **Accept the result.** Keep the system and live with the verdict.
- **Add a bridge.** State the new principle needed to avoid the result.
- **Narrow the claim.** Admit that the system does less than first claimed.

Every repair returns to the debt ledger. A bridge cannot be introduced as a free resource.

## The inference ladder

The manuscript distinguishes these conclusions:

1. A belief has a causal history.
2. A formation process was unreliable in this case.
3. A premise bears against the content of a claim.
4. A whole class of claims faces a burden of proof.
5. A whole class of claims is false.

The book may climb only when the required additional premises are stated. In particular:

- disagreement does not entail error theory;
- evolutionary influence does not entail falsehood;
- social construction does not entail worthlessness;
- lack of motivation does not by itself show lack of reasons;
- moral non-realism does not entail global skepticism about evidence or practical reasoning;
- rejection of basic desert does not erase agency, danger, trust, repair, or accountability.

## Terminology

**Objective moral fact**: a fact about moral status whose truth is not constituted merely by the actual approval, preference, commitment, convention, command, or procedure of any person or group.

**Objective moral system**: an organized account of moral statuses, their grounds, and their implications that claims at least some stance-independent truth and practical authority.

**Coherence**: success in paying the explanatory debts generated by the system's own claims without contradiction, equivocation, arbitrary exception, hidden selector, or circular support.

**Authority**: the purported capacity of a consideration or rule to count in favor of action for an agent who does not already endorse it. Authority is not identical to motivation, social pressure, legal jurisdiction, punishment, or power.

**Value**: an object of care, attachment, protection, pursuit, aversion, admiration, or identity for an agent or community. Values are treated as psychologically and socially real without assuming that they are stance-independent properties.

**Primitive**: a starting response or concern that supplies practical orientation without being inferred from a prior moral theory. Examples include pain aversion, attachment, fear, compassion, resentment of domination, delight, curiosity, loyalty, and the desire for agency or security. “Primitive” means starting point, not simple, infallible, universal, or beyond criticism.

## Prose

- Address the reader as an intelligent collaborator.
- Prefer concrete cases before theory names.
- Use philosophical labels after the underlying move is understood.
- Avoid treating confidence as evidence or confusion as profundity.
- Keep metaphors stable: construction, debt, bridge, inspection, translation.
- Do not use scare quotes around every occurrence of “moral.” Explain the contested use directly.
- Do not equate skepticism with cynicism, apathy, selfishness, or political quietism.
- Do not promise emotional liberation as a logical consequence. Some readers will experience loss.

## Exercise grammar

Every substantive chapter should contain most of the following:

- **Build** — add a clause to the Candidate System.
- **Define** — prohibit a key word from doing multiple jobs unnoticed.
- **Choose** — force an ordering where a slogan leaves several options open.
- **Pressure case** — isolate a structural seam.
- **Steelman** — write the strongest answer on behalf of the candidate.
- **Debt entry** — name what still needs grounding.
- **Transfer** — apply the insight to a real personal or public dispute.
- **Revision** — keep, amend, narrow, or remove the clause.

Exercises should produce artifacts that carry forward. By the end, the reader should possess a visible history of the attempted construction and know exactly where its objective ambitions were lost.

## The final turn

The constructive ending must not say that policy follows directly from emotion. The sequence is:

> felt response → named stake → stated value → factual claim → proposed rule → institutional safeguard → review

Emotions reveal salience; they do not certify facts, settle scope, aggregate competing interests, or immunize a group from manipulation. Evidence corrects factual beliefs. Procedures coordinate people whose values do not fully converge. Safeguards constrain power. Revision responds to error.

The final admonition is therefore:

> Stop looking for compromise between abstract moral systems as though each were a sovereign authority entitled to a share of the law. Ask instead what people concretely fear, love, need, resist, protect, and hope for. Begin public reasoning with those human stakes, make tradeoffs explicit, and build revisable institutions without pretending that a compromise has been certified by a moral realm.
