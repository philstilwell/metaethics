The error would be a beginning, not an ending.

After moral facts, what comes next?

Suppose there are no objective moral facts. What should we do with moral language? Nine choices test how you would speak, teach, promise, blame, govern, reform, and persuade.

Begin with the premise ↓
03

The working premise

“No action has an objective moral status.”

Harms, promises, laws, values, trust, love, and conflict would still be real. The question is how to talk about them.

The “what next?” problem

Rejecting objective moral facts does not choose a language policy for us.

Retire

“Stop making literal moral claims.”

Describe harm, rules, promises, aims, and opposition directly instead of calling things objectively right or wrong.

Preserve without belief

“Keep moral talk as fiction.”

Drop ordinary moral belief but knowingly retain moral language through make-believe or non-believing acceptance.

Preserve belief

“Continue ordinary moral practice.”

Keep ordinary moral belief and talk in daily life even when careful reflection says those beliefs are false.

Replace or reinterpret

“Give the words a clearer job.”

Use them openly for commitments, named procedures, agreements, or practical shorthand.

What exactly are we assuming?

Treat the premise as settled only for this lab: there are no moral properties that make an action objectively right, wrong, required, forbidden, or deserved regardless of every person’s values and standards. The lab does not claim to prove that premise.

It also does not assume that pain, agreements, legal rules, social pressure, love, or human commitments disappear. The answers are not six theories at the same level. Abolitionism, fictionalism, and conservationism are classic answers about whether to retire moral thought, preserve it without belief, or preserve belief. The other three are broad ways to replace or reinterpret the practice. They can overlap in real life.

Read this carefully

Losing objective facts would not remove the need to choose.

Every response still depends on aims: honesty, cooperation, protection, freedom, trust, stability, or something else people value. This lab maps how you would use language after the premise. It does not turn those aims into objective obligations or prove that one response must govern every case.
