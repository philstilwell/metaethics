What case does “assisted dying” mean in this lab?

Unless a question says otherwise, the phrase means one invented, narrow policy: an adult is expected to die from an incurable physical illness within six months; can understand, compare, and communicate the choice; makes two voluntary requests at least fourteen days apart; is separately assessed by two clinicians for eligibility and signs of pressure; receives information about palliative care; and, if approved, personally takes prescribed life-ending medication. A clinician may refuse to take part.

The lab separates that policy from a clinician directly administering medication and from eligibility based only on psychiatric suffering. These details are teaching stipulations, not a claim about the best law. The dialogue does not take a side or claim that either speaker’s predictions are true.

Quick guide to the few technical words
Empirical or checkable
A claim about what happens in the world and can be supported or weakened by observation, records, or experiments.
Standing
Being included as someone whose interests must enter the decision—not a guarantee that those interests will win.
Priority
The order given to included interests or values when they cannot all be fully satisfied.
Practical attitude
What someone favors, rejects, intends, or is prepared to act for—not merely a report about what is true.
Decision procedure
The process used to choose a rule: who takes part, who has authority, and how the result can be challenged or reviewed.
Objective moral claim
A claim that something is morally allowed or forbidden independently of what every person, law, or society approves.

Use the method anywhere

Eight questions for your next real argument

Do not mark a layer as present merely because it is common in similar debates. Ask what the speakers actually said, then mark unknowns as unknown.

  1. 01
    Same subject?

    What exact act, policy, group, and key words does each person mean?

  2. 02
    Different predictions?

    Which observable claims conflict, what evidence bears on them, and what uncertainty remains?

  3. 03
    Same field of concern?

    Who is affected, and whose interests does each person say should receive standing in the decision?

  4. 04
    Different priorities?

    Which values do they share, and which one leads when those values cannot all be satisfied?

  5. 05
    Opposed plans?

    What does each person favor, reject, intend, or ask the group to do?

  6. 06
    Different public rules?

    What common rule would each side build, including its scope, safeguards, and exceptions?

  7. 07
    Different ways to decide?

    Who participates, who has authority, what reasons are heard, and how can the decision be reviewed?

  8. 08
    An objective moral claim?

    Is anyone claiming a moral truth independent of every approval, and what could make that claim true or knowable?

The main lesson

A decision can be reached without every disagreement disappearing.

Better evidence may narrow predictions. Clearer words may end a verbal dispute. Deliberation may expose a compromise, and a public procedure may produce a rule. None of those results by itself proves an objective moral fact. Conversely, rejecting objective moral facts does not erase conflicts over suffering, interests, commitments, or which rules people will build together.
