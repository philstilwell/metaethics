The surface clash

“Assisted dying is wrong.” “No, it isn’t.”

Those sentences conflict. By themselves, they do not tell us what kind of conflict produced them.

The decoder’s rule

Do not ask one method to solve every kind of disagreement.

Check

Facts and predictions

Records and studies can test some claims, although uncertain evidence may not settle them completely.

Clarify

Words and scope

People must identify the same policy before their verdicts can be compared cleanly.

Work through

Interests, priorities, and plans

Evidence can inform these disputes, but it cannot choose everyone’s priorities for them.

Examine

Claims about moral facts

A claim of objective moral truth is a further metaethical claim, not a label for every unresolved conflict.

What case does “assisted dying” mean in this lab?

Unless a question says otherwise, the phrase means one narrow proposed policy: an adult with decision-making capacity has a terminal illness, makes repeated voluntary requests, receives a prescription after a regulated review, and personally takes the medication. The lab separates this from a clinician directly administering medication and from requests based only on psychiatric suffering.

The invented dialogue does not take a side on assisted dying and does not claim that either speaker’s predictions are true. It uses a serious public disagreement to teach a method: first locate the dispute, then use evidence, clarification, deliberation, institutional design, or metaethical argument where each fits.

The main lesson

A decision can be reached without every disagreement disappearing.

Better evidence may narrow predictions. Clearer words may end a verbal dispute. Deliberation may expose a compromise, and a public procedure may produce a rule. None of those results by itself proves an objective moral fact. Conversely, rejecting objective moral facts does not erase conflicts over suffering, interests, commitments, or which rules people will build together.
