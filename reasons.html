What gives a consideration practical force?

What gives you a reason?

Suppose there are no objective moral facts. What, if anything, can still count in favor of doing one thing rather than another?

The premise

Moral facts are set aside. The rest is still open.

Desires, pain, promises, evidence, relationships, procedures, and consequences remain real. The question is what—if anything—turns one of those facts into a consideration in favor of acting.

One word, three jobs

“Reason” can answer three different questions.

The lab keeps these jobs separate before asking where a reason’s force might come from.

01

What explains it?

A cause or background fact helps us understand why the action happened.

02

What moved the person?

The person acted while taking a belief or consideration as a reason.

03

What counted in favor?

A consideration supported the action, whether or not the person noticed or cared.

These roles can overlap. A person’s motivating reason often helps explain the action, but a belief can motivate even when it is false and therefore does not genuinely support what was done.

Does rejecting objective moral facts remove every kind of reason?

No conclusion follows automatically. A moral non-realist may accept desire-based reasons, reasons produced by commitments or fair procedures, non-moral reasons that apply independently of desire, or no literal reason-giving facts at all.

The lab therefore holds only one premise fixed: no objective moral facts. It does not quietly assume that all practical or rational reasons disappear.

What does “counts in favor” mean here?

It means that a fact supports choosing an action or makes a case for it. A fact can count somewhat in favor without settling the final choice; another reason may oppose it or carry more weight.

“This would achieve my goal” and “this is what I ultimately ought to do” are therefore not the same claim.

The main lesson

A world without objective moral facts does not answer the wider question about reasons.

A desire can explain an aim. A promise can create a commitment. A fair process can organize claims. Another person’s pain can matter to them. The remaining philosophical question is which of those facts, if any, genuinely counts in favor for this agent—and why.
