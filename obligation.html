The missing ought

When “wrong” only means “we disapprove.”

A community can condemn, shame, exclude, fine, or imprison. Those are real forces. But what turns any of them into a duty that still binds someone who rejects the community’s rule?

Test the claim ↓
01

The central distinction

Pressure can explain obedience without establishing authority.

“They will punish me” explains why I might comply. It does not yet explain why their rule has authority over me when punishment, agreement, and approval are removed.

Before the test

A social code and an approval-independent duty are not the same claim.

Social code

“These are our rules.”

A group can share values, demand conduct, and enforce consequences. That can be coherent and useful.

Approval-independent duty

“The rule binds beyond approval.”

This stronger claim must explain why the duty remains when people reject the rule, escape punishment, or stand outside the group.

Exactly what claim does this lab test?

An approval-independent duty does not become binding merely because a community approves it, and it does not stop binding merely because that approval disappears. It is meant to survive rejection, secrecy, and changes in public opinion.

This is not a test of every kind of moral non-realism. A non-realist may appeal to harm, consistency, fair agreement, idealized reasoning, or other standards that are more than a report of what a community currently likes. “Beyond disapproval” also does not automatically mean supernatural or mind-independent. The lab tests one narrow inference: whether the fact that a group disapproves is enough, by itself, to create a duty with wider authority.
