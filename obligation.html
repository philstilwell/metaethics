The missing ought

When “wrong” only means “we disapprove.”

A community can condemn, shame, exclude, fine, or imprison. Those are real forces. But what turns any of them into an obligation that binds someone who rejects the community’s rule?

The central distinction

Pressure can explain obedience without establishing authority.

“They will punish me” explains why I might comply. It does not yet explain why their rule is truly right or why I am morally bound to obey it.

Before the test

A social code and a universal moral claim are not the same thing.

Social code

“These are our rules.”

A group can share values, demand conduct, and enforce consequences. That can be coherent and useful.

Universal moral claim

“Everyone is truly bound.”

This stronger claim must explain why outsiders, secret violators, and principled dissenters are also obligated.

What does “universal obligation” mean here?

It means a demand that applies even when a person dislikes it, gains from breaking it, rejects the community, and can escape every punishment. This page asks whether social disapproval alone can supply that kind of authority. It does not deny that shared rules, commitments, laws, and consequences matter in human life.
