01

Causal explanation

It tells us how a conviction formed. By itself, that says nothing decisive about whether the conviction is true or well supported.

02

Reason to distrust

It shows that a process followed rewards, group labels, manipulation, or another factor that was not a good guide to the issue.

03

Argument against

It supplies evidence against what the belief says, against a premise it uses, or against the consistency of the position itself.

04

Burden shifts

It explains all present support through a process that did not track truth or relevant reasons. Independent support is now needed.

What does “genealogy” mean here?

A genealogy is an evidence-based account of how a belief, feeling, practice, or concept developed. It may involve evolution, learning, institutions, rewards, accidents, reflection, or several of these together. A plausible story is not enough: a strong genealogy needs evidence that the proposed causes actually did the work.

A genealogy can be local or broad. A local account may explain one reaction, such as a judgment driven by an in-group badge. A broad account may claim to explain a whole class of moral judgments without treating moral truth as a cause. Broader conclusions require broader evidence.

Why doesn’t an origin automatically prove falsity?

True beliefs also have causes. A teacher, frightening event, inherited tendency, or careful argument can cause someone to believe something true. To move from “this caused your belief” to “your belief is false,” an argument needs an additional bridge.

That bridge might show that the process was unreliable, that important support disappeared, or that new evidence conflicts with the belief. Even then, losing justification and being false are not the same conclusion.

Can a moral non-realist use this lab?

Yes. A conviction can be a belief about moral truth, but it can also be a commitment, plan, accepted rule, constructed conclusion, or practical attitude. You do not need to believe in approval-independent moral facts to trace what shaped it.

An attitude is not false in the way a belief can be false. Its history can still matter if it rests on mistaken factual beliefs, follows manipulation or irrelevant cues, defeats purposes you accept, or loses the support you previously gave it.

The main lesson

A cause is the beginning of the audit, not the final verdict.

A genealogy matters to confidence when evidence connects the cause to an unreliable process, removes the support a belief was receiving, or bears against what the belief actually says. Even a complete history produced without regard for moral truth most directly changes what support is still owed; further arguments are needed to conclude that every moral claim is false.
