The challenge

A society is not one voice.

It contains majorities, minorities, old promises, current laws, personal commitments, measurable effects, imagined reforms, and arguments about moral truth. “Society approves” identifies only one of these.

Four routes through the problem

First ask: criticism by whose lights?

The eight exact grounds used later belong to four broad routes. The routes are different, not higher and lower steps.

01

Actual approval

Report what today’s majority or a real subcommunity accepts. The next question is why that group—and not another—sets the standard.

02

Society’s own terms

Show that a practice conflicts with the society’s stated rule or with a right it already promised. This is criticism from inside its commitments.

03

Human reasons and procedures

State a commitment, document harm or lost control, or defend a fairer agreement. These can guide reform without pretending that approval created a cosmic command.

04

Objective moral truth

Claim that a practice is wrong independently of what any person or group approves. This is the one route that directly makes an approval-independent moral claim.

Does cultural relativism simply mean “the majority is always right”?

No. “Cultural relativism” covers several ideas. A view might say that the truth of a judgment depends on the speaker’s standard, the person being judged, a group practice, or another point of assessment. None of that automatically tells us how to draw the group boundary, how to handle overlapping groups, or whether the current majority is the right interpreter.

This lab includes current-majority approval as one clear possible ground because it is common and easy to test. It does not treat that simple view as the only sophisticated form of relativism.

Can a non-realist criticize a whole society?

Yes. A non-realist can say, for example: “I oppose this,” “this causes severe harm,” “this breaks your stated rule,” “this violates a promised right,” or “people would reject this under the fair procedure I defend.” Those claims can supply motives, evidence, leverage, and public reasons.

What a non-realist will usually avoid is quietly turning one of those human facts into an approval-independent moral property. The criticism can still be serious, costly, brave, and effective.

What will the result bars mean?

Every case answer names exactly one ground. For each ground, the result shows how many times you selected it divided by how many cases offered it. If “internal inconsistency” appears in three cases and you select it twice, its bar reads 2 / 3.

The bars are not truth probabilities, moral grades, or personality scores. Using several grounds is normal. A possible mismatch appears only when a selected case ground goes beyond the general definition of valid criticism you choose at the end.

The main lesson

Social approval is one fact about a standard. It is not the whole architecture of criticism.

Reformers can expose broken commitments, stand with a subcommunity, document harm, defend autonomy, propose a fairer procedure, speak from personal commitments, or claim approval-independent moral truth. Those routes can overlap, but they do different work. Saying exactly which work you need is more informative than calling every objection either “objectively true” or “mere disapproval.”
