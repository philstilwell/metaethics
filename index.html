Seven terms before you begin

Plain-language guide to the site’s recurring ideas

You do not need to memorize these. Open a term whenever a lab uses a phrase that feels unfamiliar.

Objective moral fact

A supposed fact about moral rightness, wrongness, duty, virtue, or desert whose truth is not created merely by what a person or group approves.

Approval-independent

Still claimed to hold when the speaker, the audience, or an entire community rejects it. This need not mean supernatural; it means that actual approval does not make the claim true.

Moral non-realism

A family of views that reject at least some ordinary claims about objective moral facts. Non-realists disagree about truth, language, reasons, rules, criticism, and what practice should remain.

Error theory

The view that ordinary moral claims try to report moral facts but systematically fail because the facts they require do not exist.

Constructivism

A family of views connecting normative conclusions to a procedure of reasoning, choice, or agreement rather than to facts fixed entirely independently of rational agents. Its relation to realism is disputed.

Basic desert

The claim that someone should receive blame, pain, or punishment simply because of what they knowingly did—not because the response protects, repairs, deters, or changes anything.

Practical reason

A consideration that counts in favor of doing something. It need not settle what to do: reasons can compete, and one can be outweighed. Philosophers disagree about what gives any consideration this force.

How the tools interact

They take apart one familiar moral sentence.

“You ought not do that because it is wrong.”

A disagreement about this sentence may concern the action, the word “wrong,” the force of “ought,” the usefulness of speaking this way, the design of a shared rule, or several hidden disputes at once. The labs keep those questions apart.

  1. 01
    The Moral Profile tests the reason.

    Why oppose this action rather than another one?

  2. 02
    The Meaning Lab tests the claim.

    What would make “wrong” accurate, sincere, or useful?

  3. 03
    The Missing Ought tests the authority.

    Why should the demand govern someone who rejects it?

  4. 04
    After Moral Facts tests the policy.

    If no objective status exists, should the sentence be retired, preserved, or revised?

  5. 05
    The Disagreement Decoder tests the layer.

    Are opponents disputing evidence, words, interests, priorities, plans, rules, procedures, or alleged moral facts?

  6. 06
    The Value Genealogy Lab tests the origin inference.

    Does a history merely explain a conviction, undermine its source, challenge its content, or shift what support is owed?

  7. 07
    The Community-Code Simulator tests construction.

    Which human reasons and procedures carry a rule when no independent moral fact settles it?

  8. 08
    The Social Criticism Lab tests reform.

    Can a dissenter use a group standard, the society’s own terms, stated human reasons, a procedure, or approval-independent truth?

  9. 09
    The Blame Laboratory tests response.

    Does a consequence protect, deter, repair, rehabilitate, set terms, revise trust, protest publicly, or make deserved suffering its point?

  10. 10
    The Reasons Lab tests the favoring relation.

    Does a consideration merely explain or motivate, or does it genuinely count in favor—and what could give it that force?
