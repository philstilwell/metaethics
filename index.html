Nine labs. Nine ways to examine moral thought.

A moral view is more than one answer.

These tools do not hand you one moral label. They separate questions people often blend together: which reasons guide a choice, what moral words mean, what could make a demand binding, what follows without objective moral facts, what two opponents actually dispute, how a conviction formed, what can support a human-built community code, how a reformer can criticize an approving society, and what remains of blame without deserved suffering.

How the tools interact

They take apart one familiar moral sentence.

“You ought not do that because it is wrong.”

A disagreement about this sentence may concern the action, the word “wrong,” the force of “ought,” the usefulness of speaking this way, the design of a shared rule, or several hidden disputes at once. The labs keep those questions apart.

  1. 01
    The Moral Profile tests the reason.

    Why oppose this action rather than another one?

  2. 02
    The Meaning Lab tests the claim.

    What would make “wrong” accurate, sincere, or useful?

  3. 03
    The Missing Ought tests the authority.

    Why should the demand govern someone who rejects it?

  4. 04
    After Moral Facts tests the policy.

    If no objective status exists, should the sentence be retired, preserved, or revised?

  5. 05
    The Disagreement Decoder tests the layer.

    Are opponents disputing evidence, words, interests, priorities, plans, rules, procedures, or alleged moral facts?

  6. 06
    The Value Genealogy Lab tests the origin inference.

    Does a history merely explain a conviction, undermine its source, challenge its content, or shift what support is owed?

  7. 07
    The Community-Code Simulator tests construction.

    Which human reasons and procedures carry a rule when no independent moral fact settles it?

  8. 08
    The Social Criticism Lab tests reform.

    Can a dissenter use a group standard, the society’s own terms, stated human reasons, a procedure, or approval-independent truth?

  9. 09
    The Blame Laboratory tests response.

    Does a consequence protect, deter, repair, rehabilitate, set terms, revise trust, protest publicly, or make deserved suffering its point?

Suggested routes

Start where your uncertainty starts.

These are useful paths, not levels to pass or a sequence designed to push everyone toward the same conclusion.

New to metaethics

Begin with a familiar argument.

Use the Decoder to take one disagreement apart, then ask what the word “wrong” is doing.

Decoder→Meaning
Skeptical about moral authority

Test the binding force.

Use The Missing Ought, then ask which form of moral practice survives its strongest result.

Obligation→After
Already a moral non-realist

Clarify language, then choose a practice.

State what moral claims do before deciding whether to keep, revise, fictionalize, or retire them.

Meaning→After
Questioning where certainty came from

Trace the source without committing a shortcut.

Map possible influences, then learn when genealogy changes reliability, content, or the burden of proof.

Genealogy→Decoder
Ready to design after moral facts

Choose a language policy, then build institutions.

Decide what moral practice should remain before testing which aims, procedures, and powers can carry a public code.

After→Build
Trying to criticize your culture

Separate reform from a popularity contest.

Build a public code, then test the different grounds on which insiders can challenge the code or its deepest values.

Build→Criticize
Rethinking blame and punishment

Separate accountability from payback.

Test what authority a demand claims, then identify which practical responses survive when deserved suffering is removed.
