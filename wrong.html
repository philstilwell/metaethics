One word. Several possible jobs.

What do you mean when you say “wrong”?

You might be reporting a fact, taking a stand, applying a shared rule, using a carefully designed reasoning process, or deliberately using a helpful moral story. Seven cases reveal which meanings you use.

Begin with a sentence ↓
02

The sentence under examination

“What they did was morally wrong.”

The sentence sounds simple. The difficult question is what a speaker commits to by saying it.

Before the cases

Agreement about an action can hide disagreement about the sentence.

Describe

“This claim can be true or false.”

The speaker may be describing an independent status or applying a rule that tells us when the claim counts as true.

Commit

“I oppose this. Let us reject it.”

The sentence may primarily express a practical stance, plan, or accepted rule rather than detect a special property.

Use

“This language helps us act.”

A speaker may knowingly retain familiar moral language as a useful story because it coordinates, motivates, or simplifies.

Why are there six interpretations?

Robust moral realists, error theorists, plan- or norm-expressivists, standard-relative theorists, idealized-procedure constructivists, and revolutionary fictionalists can use the same sentence while giving very different accounts of what it does. The labels are introduced only after each answer is explained in ordinary language.

These are narrow comparison families, not complete definitions of every theory with a similar name. Relativism is not automatically moral non-realism, and philosophers disagree about where constructivism belongs. The fictionalist option measures deliberate, revolutionary fictionalism, not every form of fictionalism. The result is a map of your answers to these cases—not proof that you hold a complete philosophical theory.

Read this carefully

A change in meaning is a question, not a conviction.

People sometimes use moral language differently in private reflection, public persuasion, disagreement, and rule-making. This diagnostic marks those changes so you can decide whether they are useful context shifts, revisions, or meanings that should be made more consistent.
